# AMPLITUDE SHIFT KEYING (ASK)

---

## INTRODUCTION:

Amplitude Shift Keying (ASK) is a digital modulation technique used in communication systems to transmit digital data by varying the amplitude of a carrier signal. It is commonly applied in Frequency Division Multiplexing (FDM), where multiple users share a channel by occupying different frequency bands. In ASK, the presence or absence of the carrier or changes in its amplitude represents the digital logic levels, making it one of the simplest forms of digital modulation.

Because the envelope of an ASK signal follows the shape of the digital data, signal recovery at the receiver can be achieved using a simple envelope detector. However, this simplicity comes with a disadvantage, as ASK is highly susceptible to noise and channel interference, which can distort the signal envelope and lead to errors in data interpretation. This experiment examines the generation of ASK signals, their time-domain characteristics, and the effects of noise and channel conditions on reliable digital data transmission.

## LAB QUESTIONS AND ANSWERS:

**Q1: What is the relationship between the digital signal and the presence of the carrier in the ASK Signal?**

- The digital signal controls the presence of the carrier.
- When the digital signal is logic 1, the carrier is present at the output.
- When the digital signal is logic 0, the carrier is suppressed or turned off.

**Q2: What is the ASK signal's voltage when the digital signal is logic 0?**  

When the digital signal is logic 0, the ASK signal has approximately 0 V amplitude, since the carrier is disconnected by the analog switch.

**Q3: What feature of the ASK Signal suggests that it's an AM Signal?**  

The envelope of the ASK signal follows the shape of the digital data. The carrierâ€™s amplitude varies while its frequency remains constant, which is characteristic of amplitude modulation (AM).

**Q4: Why is the recovered digital signal not a perfect copy of the original?**

The envelope detector introduces ripple and slow rise/fall times due to the charging and discharging of the filter capacitor. Noise and filtering effects slightly distort the signal, preventing perfect reconstruction.

**Q5: What can be used to "Clean up" the recovered digital signal?**

A comparator can be used to clean up the recovered signal by restoring sharp logic transitions and fixed logic levels.

**Q6: How does the comparator turn the slow rising voltage of the recovered digital signal into sharp transitions?**

The comparator switches its output state whenever the input crosses a fixed reference voltage. This converts gradual voltage changes into fast transitions between logic high and logic low.

## CONCLUSIONS:
After analyzing the gathered data and observations about Amplitude Shift Keying (ASK), the following conclusions have been made:
- Amplitude Shift Keying (ASK) transmits digital data by switching the amplitude of a carrier signal according to the digital input.
- ASK can be easily generated using a digital control signal and an analog switch.
- The envelope of the ASK signal directly represents the digital data, allowing simple demodulation using an envelope detector.
- Envelope detection successfully recovers the baseband digital signal but introduces distortion and ripple.
- Noise and filtering effects prevent the recovered signal from being an exact replica of the original digital data.
- A comparator effectively restores the recovered signal by reshaping it into clean, well-defined logic levels
- ASK offers simplicity in implementation but is highly susceptible to noise and channel interference compared to other digital modulation techniques.
